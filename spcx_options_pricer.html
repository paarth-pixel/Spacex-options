Black-Scholes formula

Call = S·N(d₁) − K·e⁻ʳᵀ·N(d₂)
Put = K·e⁻ʳᵀ·N(−d₂) − S·N(−d₁)

Where d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T) and d₂ = d₁ − σ√T. N(x) is the cumulative normal distribution. At 100% vol, options are very expensive — the stock can plausibly double or halve before expiry.

Why options are pricey at 100% vol

With σ = 100%, one standard deviation move over 0.567 years is ±75% of the stock price. That means the market is saying there's roughly a 68% chance SPCX is anywhere between $42 and $290 by January. Options reflect this enormous uncertainty with high premiums. SPCX just IPO'd — 100% vol is realistic for a newly listed high-growth stock.

What theta means here

Theta is how much the option loses each day just from time passing — assuming the stock doesn't move. With only 207 days to expiry and high volatility, theta is large. Every day you hold a long option position costs you money even if SpaceX stays flat. This is why option sellers love high-vol stocks.

Put-call parity

For any pair of European call and put with the same strike and expiry: C − P = S − K·e⁻ʳᵀ. This must hold — if it doesn't, you can make a risk-free profit. All prices in this table satisfy parity. The difference between call and put at a given strike tells you exactly how far in or out of the money that strike is in present-value terms.

ITM / OTM / ATM explained

In the money (ITM call): strike below current stock price — already profitable if exercised now.
Out of the money (OTM call): strike above stock price — needs the stock to rise to be worth anything at expiry.
At the money (ATM): strike closest to current price — most sensitive to volatility changes.

Why today vs tomorrow matters

Even if the stock price stays exactly the same, options lose a tiny bit of value each day. The "tomorrow" column shows prices with one fewer day to expiry. The difference between today and tomorrow (holding stock price constant) is pure theta decay — the daily cost of holding the option.
